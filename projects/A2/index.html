Assignment 2 Exploratory Data Analysis

Adi Kupershmidt — adi_kup@mit.edu

Subtheme: Speculation

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. 1. What prominent trends in house flipping have occurred over the past twenty years? Can they be traced back to specific policy or regulations?

    In the final part of the "Homes for Profit: Speculation and Investment in Greater Boston" report, MAPC are recommending some policies and planning startegies to ammeliorate the residential replacement and gentrification caused by speculating investors. I think it will be interesting to look at flipping housing trends throughout the last two decades, and learn how they were influenced by policy changes, so that we can better understnad how to make more impactful policy in the future.

  2. 2. What sort of housing properties are predominantly targeted by flippers?

    I wonder if there are specific properties that are more proned to be targeted by speculative investment. A real estate agent from Boston that I met before moving into on-campus housing, told me of numerous "flipping" projects he was working on. Most of them were multi-family houses. I wonder if we could map the properties that "flippers" are looking for, perhaps we can better regulate those specific markets.

  3. 3. Who are the main investors in house flipping around Boston?

    Reading Pro Publica's article by Heather Vogell, I was astounded by the influx of private equity firms investing in multifamily apartments. I always thought huge corporates won't invest in speculating over such small ventures (such as a single lot real estate investment). I wonder if the dataset will reflect that as well - who are the main stakeholders investing in "flipping" and can they be identified and traced to few dominant players?

  4. 4. Where are the largest concentrations of flipped housing ventures in the Greater Boston area?

    Lastly, I think it's useful to understand where most real-estate speculations take place. By identifying clusters of house flipping, we can later target policies and planning strategies at specific parts of the metropolis.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

From this preliminary data exploration, I think we can identify some main characteristics of properties that are prone to be targeted by real estate speculators. Some of them are: location, a relatively big number of bedrooms, and recent building years. Moreover, the main sector investing in flipping seems to be small independent investors. This is surprising, especially in light of the article by Heather Vogell, showing private equity enterprises were responsible for most of it. I think further research is needed to fully understand this descrepency and the way previous federal policies may have effected the flipping housing market in 2010 (if they did at all), as well as what has stopped the rise of flipping in 2016.
