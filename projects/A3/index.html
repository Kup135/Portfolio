Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

Designing this visualization, my intention was to narrate the significant growth in population over a century and suggest the potential environmental consequences of this expansion. The design is meant to illustrate not just the increase in numbers but to connect it to a concept that resonates on a personal level—the idea of family generations. The comparison between 1900 and 2000 is encapsulated in the metaphor of 'sacks' filled with points, each representing demographic data. This metaphor aims to evoke the weight of the population increase and its metaphorical burden on the earth's resources.

In terms of visual encodings, I chose the sack shape to convey population data, with the size of each sack corresponding to the number of individuals in an age group. The color-coding—pink for the 1900 data and blue for 2000—was selected to differentiate clearly between the two time periods at a glance. Inverting the Y-axis was a deliberate choice to visually support the metaphor of weight, suggesting the idea of population figures as a load to be supported. This decision also subverts the typical association of 'up' with 'growth,' prompting the viewer to think differently about population increases.

However, I acknowledge that this metaphorical approach could potentially mislead the viewer. The size of the sacks, while visually striking, may not accurately represent the proportional differences in population sizes between the two years. Additionally, the individual points inside the sacks do not correspond to exact figures, which was a sacrifice I made for the sake of visual impact over precision.

Reflection

Reflecting on the initial sketching phase, it's clear that it was influential in the development of my final design. The inverted Y-axis and annotations were ideas that I retained from my initial sketches. The sketching phase also highlighted the potential misrepresentation of data through the sack metaphor, yet I chose to maintain this approach in the final design. I did so with the understanding that while the sacks might not convey exact numbers, they serve the larger narrative I aimed to tell. In retrospect, sketching was a valuable step that helped me explore creative ways to represent data, and while it revealed some limitations of my initial ideas, it also solidified my commitment to an affective, metaphor-driven approach.
